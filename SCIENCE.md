# The Science of Coherence

**The short version:** When you breathe slowly — around six breaths a minute — your
heart rate starts to swing up and down in a large, smooth wave. That wave is real,
measurable, and it reflects your nervous system settling into balance. *Coherence* is
our name for it. This page is the evidence behind the number.

## Your heartbeat isn't a metronome

A healthy heart doesn't tick at a fixed rate. The tiny changes in time between beats —
**heart rate variability (HRV)** — are a window onto your autonomic nervous system, the
automatic controller behind stress and calm. Higher breath-driven variability generally
reflects healthy **vagal (parasympathetic) tone** — the "rest and recover" side of the
nervous system — and is linked to better attention and emotional regulation.<sup>[1,4]</sup>

## Why slow breathing grows the wave

Two mechanisms combine. First, **respiratory sinus arrhythmia**: your heart naturally
speeds up as you inhale and slows as you exhale, an effect carried by the vagus
nerve.<sup>[1]</sup> Second, the **baroreflex** — the loop that stabilizes blood pressure —
has a natural resonance at about **0.1 Hz**. Breathe at roughly six breaths per minute and
you drive the system right at that resonant frequency: heart rate and blood pressure fall
into a 180° push-pull, and the oscillation is amplified far beyond
normal.<sup>[2,5]</sup> The result is a big, clean, sine-like swing in your heart rate.
Each person's exact resonance sits between about 4.5 and 6.5 breaths per minute.<sup>[2]</sup>

## What the coherence score measures

In that resonant state, almost all of your heart-rate variation collects into a single,
tall, narrow peak near **0.1 Hz** in the frequency spectrum.<sup>[2]</sup> Coherence is
measured by looking for exactly that peak. A high score means your heart rhythm has become
smooth, ordered, and dominated by that ~0.1 Hz wave — a physiological signature of breath,
heart, and blood pressure moving together.

## What it does for you

Slow-paced breathing / HRV biofeedback is one of the better-studied self-regulation
techniques:

- A meta-analysis of **24 studies (484 people)** found **large reductions in stress and
  anxiety** (Hedges' *g* ≈ 0.8).<sup>[3]</sup>
- A broader review of **58 studies** found consistent **small-to-moderate benefits**,
  strongest for anxiety, depression, and anger.<sup>[4]</sup>
- Trials also show measurable physiological effects, including increased **baroreflex
  gain** and modest **blood-pressure reductions**.<sup>[5]</sup>
